My Design Process

Process is a designer's lifeline.

While I often tweak, here is the backbone I rely on.

  • Discover
    They say 'starting is half the battle'. This is the time when you first get a taste of a project, get in touch with the stakeholders, learn about the vision, the strategy, the issues, the needs. How? Focus Groups, Interviews & Surveys, Usability Audits, Usability Goals, Gap Assesments, Requirement Gathering and more.
  • Define
    Now you need to extract, process and refine information you have discovered into Storyboards, User Profiles, Requirement Analysis, User Experience Definitions, Benchmarking ... You have to establish what your role and efforts will be. These are foundations for any good work you can do on a project. If you won't talk to people, don't be a designer.
  • Design
    You now know enough to start producing. Designing Wireframes, Prototypes, making fully functional Designs or just Concepts, this is where you start formulating solutions to needs. And, honestly, it is a really fun step.
  • Develop
    It's time to bring ideas to life. A good design is nothing without thoughtful execution. So make sure you dot all your "i"s and cross all your "t"s. Seriously, typos in code are the devil.
  • Deploy
    You have made it this far, which means you probably had to test something in Internet Explorer, for which you deserve a pat on the back. Yet the work is not over. You need to check that all is going according to plan. Data is key. Do your A/B Testing, User Testing, User Interviews, stare at your Analytics. If you see issues, time to go back a few steps, as far as you need to understand where your product is lacking. Now, is all looking good? Hold on, there's one more step left.
  • Drive
    "Time Always Wins". So you've got a kick-ass product that everyone loves... for now. People change, markets transform, companies adapt, customers sway and needs evolve. Keep your eyes on your metrics (and your competitors), talk to your users, and go back to the brainstorm and/or drawing boards when needed. But maybe have a beer first — #treatyoself.

My 8 Principles

I never stop learning, these are some things I have picked up on the way.

  • 1 The User Is King

    As a designer, I create material. Experience is users' subjective reactions to my creations. My work is only as good as they think it is.

  • 2 Design Is Holistic

    Every part of a project needs consistent design efforts for the user’s experience to be complete.

  • 3 Be Invisible

    Great visuals, performance, discoverability & learnability, efficient workflows, emotional connection and, finally, delight. The best design is one the user adopts instantly, enthusiastically and effortlessly.

  • 4 Put Time On Your Side

    Good design stands the test of time, and is to be changed only when necessary, but should be absolutely changed when necessary. Equally, stability is required to give users time to learn how to incrementally do more and better with the same product.

  • 5 Conversations Matter

    Be it survey feedback, live testing, interviews, any information exchanged with users contains signals and potential breakthroughs to better a design.

  • 6 Simplicity Is Misunderstood

    ‘Minimalist’, ‘pure’, ‘organic’ designs are not what will satisfy users. Clarity is what they need. Be honest and accessible in your design, not to show off your skills or indulge your taste, but because it helps the user.

  • 7 It's The Economy, Stupid!

    A great design should always aim to better an experience, and one of the consequences should be and influx of users or a growth in demand. If no one is using a product, there are questions to ask about its design.

  • 8 Designers Just Wanna Have Fun

    A happy designer is a better designer. To have the patience, the openness, the empathy needed, we must first take care of ourselves and make sure we’re happy before making things for others.

Projects

  • Booking.com
    UX Challenge
    Amsterdam, Netherlands
    2016
    Summary

    I was challenged by Booking B.V. to find actionable changes to quickly deploy to their main website.

    Discover

    I browsed Booking.com extensively on multiple platforms (large, medium and small screens).

    I soon discovered that while on the large-viewport version of their homepage they are pushing for the installation of their mobile app in 4 different locations in 4 different ways, they are not adverstising it as strongly on smaller screens/mobile devices.

    Furthering my research, I compared their choices to their main competitor, Expedia.

    Compared to Booking.com, on Expedia.com mobile version the "install our mobile application" section is placed higher in the page. Its design incorportates mobile app stores graphics, allowing for easy purpose identification by visitors. In contrast, Booking.com chose not to use any visual clues that this is indeed an opportunity to install their mobile app, and relies instead on the text content alone.

    Define

    As of now, mobile applications offer unique ways to interact with users that websites cannot provide : integration with mobile devices OSes (dashboards, sharing capabilities, access to device data), notifications, payment integrations, better data storage capabilities, better persistent authentication as well better visual presentation and performance. All things equal, these capabilities allow companies to provide an all-together better experience to users, translating into a larger share of users becoming "convertible" and a higher conversion rate.

    For these reasons, it makes sense to push for installation of a mobile app on large viewports, and it makes even more sense to promote this to users viewing Booking.com on their mobile devices, as the path to installation is direct and much shorter, on average 3 taps.

    Design

    I recommend altering the design to increase its appeal and visibility and do a multivariate testing operation with :

    • Current design + current position
    • New design + current position
    • New design + new position
    • Current design + new position.
    Deploy

    I suggest tracking :

    • Number of clicks on the section / Unique visitors
    • Number of installs per click (conversion rate)

  • Bunq
    ATM Design
    Amsterdam, Netherlands
    2016
    Summary

    I haven been a Bunq customer since early 2016. Their product is absolutely fantastic in terms of interface design and experience, especially within the banking industry.

    I explored how their innovative approach would translate into an ATM design.

    Transparent Customer Differenciation

    Instead of having a first screen asking ATM users whether they are Bunq customers or not, I choose to rely on their card to make the distinction.

    • Benefits
    • Eliminating a step in the identification/authentication interface,
    • Combining this step with the necessary card verification step,
    • Allowing holders of Bunq cards to use both their Bunq cards and non-Bunq cards, and be offered appropriate options without the need to actively identify themselves (transparent process).
    Full-service Interface

    My design contains a full-featured customer interface, allowing the user to view a summary of accounts, detailed operations on each account, cards information. It also allows operations such as withdrawal and deposit to accounts, linking cards to different accounts, and in case of loss/theft the freezing of cards and/or accounts, unlinking of the user’s phone and direct call to the Bunq Customer Center.

    • Benefits
    • Extend the excellent experience of the mobile application to the ATM medium.
    • Offer substitute services in case of loss of mobile phone
    Augmented Idle/Lock Screen

    Most ATMs make little use of the idle/lock screen. This screen is displayed when the ATM is not in operation. It is prime visual space, as it is the most often displayed screen, and can be viewed by passersby, who willalways outnumber active users, without the need for any action on their part.

    I opt to make more use of this space, by displaying time and location specific messages such as “Time for a snack? Get some cash here!” for a machine set in a transport system (subway, train station ...) in late afternoon,or “Start the day right with some coffee! Get some cash here !” at 7am in a high-traffic location or 10am in a leisure area on a weekend.

    • Benefits
    • These messages can be remotely dispatched, tested against usage metrics, and help increase usage of the ATM and in turn brand awareness and potential customer acquisition for Bunq.
    • They also reflect the brand’s positioning as a “fresh and friendly” bank
    • For everyday passers-by, they can become part of their ritual, surprising them with new messages during their commute, increasing brand familiarity.
    • Co-branding is also possible, seasonal and other types of time and location specific messages can help increase usage, overall brand-awareness/recognition and brand familiarity for Bunq.
    Multiple Authentication Methods

    Bunq customers are used to advanced authentication methods within the mobile application : face recognition, fingerprint reading (TouchID), SMS confirmation codes, identity document scanning…

    Users might forget their PIN or not want to type it in for privacy concerns, they might prefer higher security biometry methods such as face and voice recognition, or they might prefer swiping their mobile phones over an NFC reader for convenience.

    To accommodate these scenarios, I choose to offer 5 different methods. They are available to users who have inserted a Bunq card as they all require prior information entry (face/voice print, mobile phone identifier/number…)

    • Benefits
    • Allowing customers to choose the authentication method they prefer : This step can be tedious and generate friction in the workflow. Also, users are best equipped to choose their own compromise between security, ease of use and their technical/practical abilities.
  • Personal Project
    Itinerary Assistant
    Paris, France
    2015
    Summary

    I created a simple and effective transport routing web app, accessible at https://julesthivent.com/itinerary-assistant/.

    Discover

    A friend of mine complained he was finding routing/itinerary services confusing to use.

    He also wanted to be able to use the same interface on any device, without having to install applications.

    I looked at Google Map services, Apple Map services and local competitors, yet I could not find an interface with the level of simplicity that my friend required.

    Define

    I first discussed my friend’s issues with other itinerary assistant options (mobile applications, websites).

    I took in his requirements and I drew a simple interface, explaining to him how I could use Google APIs to improve the quality of his experience (auto-complete, ability to go from any address to any address, contextual information on itineraries, updates on disturbances…).

    Design

    The design is split in two columns.

    On the left, a 75% width column contains a Google Maps interactive map (of Paris, by default). The map has custom styles to soften map features and make the public transport layer more visible.

    On the right is a form, where you can input departure and arrival locations in two text fields. Both fields have auto-complete.

    You then select a departure time, or leave the default option which is a departure within the next 15 minutes.

    When the user clicks “Find Itinerary”, routes are displayed in a list format as well as on the interactive map.

    Deploy

    I put together the project in a day and had my friend test it.

    I noticed that although he used the product succesfully, he was required to manually choose the departure time using the select menu all the way from “00:00” to his desired depature time, even when he wanted to leave immediately.

    I subsequently update the code to include a time selection function that selects the next quarter hour as default departure time, eliminating 2 taps in that use case.
